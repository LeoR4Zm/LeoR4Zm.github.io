Interactive narratives

How games tell stories

Games mix cutscenes, dialogue, player choice, and environmental clues to tell stories that respond to what the player does.

Linear vs. branching stories

Some games follow a single, carefully directed narrative, while others branch into different paths and endings. Both approaches can be effective, depending on the experience they aim to create.

  • Linear stories emphasize pacing and cinematic moments
  • Branching stories react to choices and encourage replays
  • Side quests and optional content deepen worldbuilding

Environmental storytelling

The world itself can act as a storyteller. Level design, props, notes, and visual details quietly hint at what happened before the player arrived.

  • Abandoned rooms, graffiti, and ruins suggest past events
  • Lighting and sound guide emotion without a single line of dialogue
  • Small details reward players who like to explore slowly

Story-driven games & Wildermyth

Some games are built almost entirely around narrative choices and character arcs. Wildermyth is a great example: it blends tactical combat with comic-style story panels, letting characters age, change, and carry scars from one campaign to the next.

  • Procedural events create unique stories for each playthrough
  • Characters can retire, form families, and return in later campaigns
  • Player decisions shape both the plot and how heroes develop over time
